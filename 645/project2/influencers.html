Sergey Brin (Co-founder of Google)

Sergey Brin was born on August 21, 1973, in Moscow, Russia. He came from a well-off family with both parents having college degrees and jobs. He and his parents (Micheal and Engenia Brin) moved to the US in 1979 when he was about six (6) years old because of the discrimination his dad faced being a Jew. His family settled in Maryland, where his dad took a job at the University of Maryland teaching math.

Growing up, Sergey had excellent skills in math and science because of his background. He breezed through middle school easily and spent three (3) years in high school and college respectively. Sergey was awarded a National Science Foundation scholarship that enabled him to apply and get admitted to Stanford University, where he met Larry Page as a second-year student.

Sergey and Larry, along with four other graduate students, spent a vast majority of their time in Larry’s office (room 360) at the newly created computer center that was named after Bill Gates. They created a home away from home with hanging plants, a sleeping pad, and a piano that connected to a computer. (Sergey Brin and Larry Page: Founders of Google - Stephanie Sammartino McPherson - Google Books, n.d.) The duo began Google as a research project with a third founder, Scott Hassan who wrote the code for the search engine but later went on to do robotics. The search engine (BackRub) was based on an algorithm called PageRank that worked better than the Status quo at the time. It ranked websites based on how many pages they had and how relevant the pages that linked back to the original site were. The work of Robin Li, the creator of RankDex, was mentioned in the PageRank patent as having influenced the creation of PageRank. The duo collaborated on their first paper on the idea with Rajeev Motwani and Terry Winograd, and Hassan and Steremberg are also credited with being critical to the development of Google.

The search engine was later changed from BackRub to Google. On September 15, 1997, the domain name "www.google.com" was registered, and the corporation was formed on September 4, 1998. They were able to acquire around $1,000,000 from friends and family, as well as an investment from an Angel Investor, and opened their first shop in Menlo Park, California.

Steve Jobs (Co-founder of Apple)

Steve Jobs founded Apple alongside Steve Wozniak in 1976. Jobs had the vision to bring computing to the mass market through beautiful and accessible design, branding Apple’s computers as “the computer for the rest of us”. The Macintosh computer is responsible for bringing us the mouse and keyboard as well as the window based graphical interface that we use today. It is this window interface that allowed Tim Berners-Lee to create a widely accessible hypertext internet that we know as the world wide web.

Upon resuming work as the permanent CEO of Apple in 2000, Jobs began to shift the intent of the company back to his vision. He took inspiration from Mies van der Rohe’s “Less is more” approach to design. As such, Steve Jobs began to insist that product design must drive the engineering at Apple rather than the other way around stating “We’re the only company that owns the whole widget—the hardware, the software, and the operating system. We can take full responsibility for the user experience. We can do things that the other guys can’t do”. It is this user-first philosophy that helped to propel the design space within tech.

Jobs’ next leap in innovation with regards to the web came in 2007. The iPhone hit the market and instantly changed the way we interact with the web. With practical touch screens, mobile phones soon became the primary environment for many’s web experiences.

The success of Apple can be attributed to Steve Jobs’ commitment to not just the functionality of its products, but also the aesthetics. Jobs aimed to deliver products that delighted its customers. As a result, he was able to help move the web and computing from a professional tool to an everyday necessity.

Tim Berners-Lee (Inventor of the World Wide Web (WWW))

The inventor of the web as we know it today. Berners-Lee began to develop his idea for the world wide web in 1989. He had been working for CERN for five years and wanted to implement a hypertext based system for CERN’s massive internal network of data and documentation. At the time hypertext was a niche software method that was primarily restricted to closed systems. What Tim Berners-Lee envisioned, however, was a unified hypertext standard and protocols that could be accessible to anyone connected on the internet.

Through his work in creating the world wide web, Berners-Lee needed to instate unifying standards that would ensure compatibility regardless of operating systems or hardware. As a result he created three internet standards that persist hrough today. These standards are HyperText Markup Language (HTML), HyperText Transfer Protocol (HTTP), and Uniform Resource Locator (URL).

Upon seeing his proposed platform, CERN allocated time for Berners-Lee to begin coding the world wide web. Tim along with a team mostly of interns was able to cleverly combine and adapt established toolsets in order to develop and release a world wide web prototype software package on the internet within ten months time.
